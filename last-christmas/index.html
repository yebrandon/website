Last Christmas

Holiday cheer and striking social commentary

Last Christmas is a 2019 romantic comedy directed by Paul Feig, written by Emma Thompson, Greg Wise, and Bryony Kimmings, and starring Emilia Clarke and Henry Golding. It is also the week 11 screening for Dan Vena's FILM225 course at Queen's University, and I've decided that to fufill my prompt for the week, I would express my opinion that Last Christmas is a post-postfeminist film by showing a few examples of post-postfeminism in the film using a website!

Post-postfeminism in Last Christmas

Love

The unity of the heterosexual monogamous couple is a common trope amongst romcom endings, and enforces the agenda of capitalist and consumerist society. Last Christmas subverts this trope by showing our main character achieve love and happiness through a variety of different means outside of the standard romcom couple, as she expresses love within her family, friends, community, and with Tom, who is no longer alive but who had saved her life with a heart transplant.

Social Inequity

Many postfeminist films are critiqued for not addressing the larger social, political, and cultural problems that lead to inequity, yet Last Christmas takes place in a post-Brexit setting and explicitly highlights the kind of tension that the political atmosphere creates through the life of our protagonist.

Capitalism and Consumerism

The "Girl Power" style movements that can be seen taking shape during the time of postfeminism ties female empowerment together with wealth and purchasing power, which is problematic as it pushes capitalism and consumerism without addressing how these ideas contribute to larger structural inequities in society. In contrast, Last Christmas presents the idea that one can be happy and loved without needing to be wealthy and spend a lot of money through our protagonist's interactions with her local homeless shelter.
